Ocean Acidification: A Silent Threat to Marine Life

The ocean is one of Earth's greatest carbon sinks, absorbing about 30% of the carbon dioxide produced by human activity. But this comes at a steep cost a series of chemical reactions that are fundamentally altering the chemistry of our seas, with consequences that will ripple through marine ecosystems for centuries.



How Acidification Works

When carbon dioxide (CO₂) dissolves in seawater, it forms carbonic acid. This acid then breaks apart into bicarbonate and hydrogen ions. The increase in hydrogen ions is what makes the water more acidic. Since the Industrial Revolution, ocean pH has dropped from 8.2 to approximately 8.1 a seemingly small shift that actually represents a 26% increase in acidity, because pH is measured on a logarithmic scale.

At the current rate of CO₂ emissions, ocean pH could fall to 7.95 by 2100 a level not seen on Earth for more than 20 million years.

The Impact on Marine Life

High acidity reduces the availability of carbonate ions the essential building blocks that marine organisms use to form shells and skeletons. The consequences are already being felt across species:

  • Corals: Coral skeletons weaken and dissolve faster than they can be rebuilt. Entire reef systems home to roughly 25% of all ocean species are at risk of collapsing within decades.
  • Shellfish: Oysters, mussels, clams, and crabs struggle to form shells in acidic conditions. Oyster hatcheries in the Pacific Northwest have already reported widespread larval die-offs directly linked to acidification.
  • Pteropods: These tiny free-swimming sea snails, a critical food source for salmon, mackerel, and whales, have been found with visibly dissolving shells in waters off the U.S. West Coast.
  • Fish Behaviour: Studies show that acidic water impairs the sensory systems of young fish, making it harder for them to detect predators, find food, and navigate back to reef habitats.

Threat to Coral Reefs

Coral reefs are often called the rainforests of the sea yet they cover less than 1% of the ocean floor while supporting an estimated 1 million species. Ocean acidification works alongside rising sea temperatures to cause mass coral bleaching events, where stressed corals expel the algae living in their tissues and turn ghostly white. Without those algae, corals slowly starve. The Great Barrier Reef has experienced five mass bleaching events since 1998, with the most severe occurring in 2016, 2017, 2020, and 2022.

Consequences for Food Security

More than 1 billion people worldwide depend on seafood as their primary source of animal protein, and the global fishing and aquaculture industry employs over 600 million people. As acidification undermines shellfish populations and disrupts marine food webs, coastal communities particularly in developing nations - face serious threats to both nutrition and livelihoods. Salmon and tuna populations are also indirectly affected, as the small organisms they feed on disappear.

The Connection to Climate Change

Ocean acidification and climate change share the same root cause: the burning of fossil fuels and the resulting rise in atmospheric CO₂. They are often described as a "deadly duo" acting simultaneously on the same ecosystems. Warming waters reduce the ocean's ability to absorb CO₂, meaning the more the planet heats, the less the ocean can help offset our emissions. This creates a dangerous feedback loop that accelerates both crises.

What Scientists and Governments Are Doing

Researchers are monitoring ocean pH through global networks like the Global Ocean Acidification Observing Network (GOA-ON), tracking changes across ocean basins in real time. Some scientists are experimenting with adding alkaline minerals to seawater locally to temporarily buffer acidity around vulnerable reefs though scaling such interventions remains a challenge. The most effective long-term solution, scientists agree, is a rapid and deep reduction in global CO₂ emissions.

Several nations have incorporated ocean acidification targets into their national climate commitments under the Paris Agreement, and the UN Decade of Ocean Science (2021–2030) has prioritised acidification research as a key focus area.

What You Can Do

Addressing ocean acidification requires systemic change, but individual actions collectively build political and social pressure for that change. You can help by:

  • Reducing your carbon footprint through transport, diet, and energy choices.
  • Supporting organisations working on marine conservation and climate policy.
  • Choosing sustainably sourced seafood to reduce additional stress on vulnerable species.
  • Staying informed and raising awareness in your community about the link between emissions and ocean health.
